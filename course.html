Hardware Assurance Programme · Pre-Work Curriculum

Course

Four weeks of structured reading and discussion to bring the cohort to a shared starting point on Day 1. Roughly 12-15 hours of work split across foundations, your chosen track, depth plus small-group discussions, and a light landing week.

Week 1 · T-4 · Foundations

Foundations

Everyone reads the same three core readings. By end of Week 1 the whole cohort can have a substantive conversation about why AI verification matters, what mechanisms have been proposed, and where the technical infrastructure is today.

Core readings (all participants)

READ 01 90 min Strategic frame

Hardware-Enabled Mechanisms for Verifying Responsible AI Development

Aarne, Fist, Withers, Maas, et al. (2024)

The canonical paper on HEMs. Read for the strategic frame: why hardware-rooted verification is the missing layer in current AI governance, and the taxonomy of mechanisms (location verification, network verification, offline licensing, workload verification). This is the paper everyone in the cohort will reference.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-4.
READ 02 2–3 hours Technical taxonomy

FlexHEG Report — Project 2 highlights

Future of Life Institute and FlexHEG consortium (2025)

The most concrete technical roadmap published. Focus on the sections covering network-tap design, secure enclosure architectures, and the trust-establishment protocol between prover and verifier. Skim the parts on adversary modelling. Read carefully the parts on what is buildable today vs in 5+ years.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-4.
READ 03 60 min Technical anchor

Verifying LLM Inference to Prevent Model Weight Exfiltration

Rinberg, Karvonen, Hoover, Reuter, Warr (2025)

Read for the technique itself (Fixed-Seed Sampling Likelihood, Token-DiFR) and as an example of how to think about verification as a statistical problem. Daniel Reuter is one of HAP's residents. This is current resident work, not historical reference. Bring questions for him.

→ Paper on arXiv (2511.02620)
READ 04 30 min Optional · accessible explainer

Defending Against Model Weight Exfiltration Through Inference Verification

Rinberg et al. — LessWrong post (2025)

The same Rinberg paper made more accessible. If you are reading the original paper and finding it dense, read this first.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-4.
Opening call · mid-Week 1

Opening call (90 minutes)

Residents introduce themselves (5 min each, background and what they are excited to discuss). Each participant introduces themselves (2 min, background and what you want to work on). Then open discussion on the readings for the rest of the call.

You do not need to have your project picked yet. You should know which open-problem area pulls you most.

Week 2 · T-3 · Your Track

Your Track

In Week 1 you saw the whole landscape. In Week 2 you go deep on one of five open-problem tracks. Pick the track closest to your background and curiosity. You can change later — these are not binding.

Track A · Tamper-evident enclosures

For people drawn to the physical-security side of HAP: how do you build enclosures around 1,200W liquid-cooled accelerators that a state actor with physical access cannot tamper with undetectably.

READ A1 45 min

AI Security Forum RFD 006 — Secure Enclosure (SCIF) for AI Clusters

James Petrie (HAP resident) (2024)

The shortest, most concrete framing of the tamper-evident enclosure problem at HAP scale. James will be in the room. Bring questions about why current SCIF designs do not survive the thermal envelope of frontier accelerators.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-3.
READ A2 60 min

Banking HSM design overview

Pick any accessible introduction

HSMs have solved the small-scale tamper-evidence problem for thirty years. The interesting question is what transfers when you go from a 50W device to a 1,200W liquid-cooled accelerator. Come to the programme with three things you think transfer and one thing you think does not.

Suggested introductions circulated with track assignment.
READ A3 60–90 min

Nuclear monitoring tamper-evidence literature

One accessible paper — IAEA safeguards handbook or a Sandia survey

The other domain with deep tamper-evidence engineering. What did IAEA learn from forty years of treaty inspection? Frangible seals, optical fibres, polymer films — what is portable to AI hardware.

Specific reading assigned per participant.

Track B · Mutually trusted attestation hardware

For people drawn to the trust-establishment problem: how do you build hardware that both prover and verifier trust, given each has different threat models and incentives.

READ B1 30 min

AI Security Forum RFD 008 — Attested Logging with Existing Hardware

James Petrie (2024)

Frames the attestation question through existing-hardware lenses. The starting point for understanding what is already shipped and where the gaps are.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-3.
READ B2 45 min

Trail of Bits — Confidential Computing blog series

Trail of Bits (ongoing)

Industry-grade analysis of what TEEs actually deliver vs claim. Read at least two posts. The audit-focused ones are best.

→ Trail of Bits blog
READ B3 60 min

WireTap, Battering RAM, or TEE.fail

Pick one (2025)

Recent attacks redefining the TEE threat model. They matter because the "trust the silicon" assumption is now substantively contested. Bring one specific attack mechanism you find most worrying for AI verification.

Links to each attack paper circulated with track assignment.

Track C · Inference verification at scale

For people drawn to the statistical and cryptographic angles: how do you verify that a deployed AI model matches what was safety-tested, without disrupting workloads or revealing weights.

READ C1 60 min Re-read from Week 1

Verifying LLM Inference to Prevent Model Weight Exfiltration

Rinberg, Karvonen, Hoover, Reuter, Warr (2025)

Now read more carefully. Focus on Section 4 (the FSSL estimators) and Section 5 (the empirical results). Note where the authors are most and least confident.

→ Paper on arXiv
READ C2 30 min

Defeating Nondeterminism in LLM Inference

Thinking Machines (2025)

Concurrent and complementary to Rinberg et al. Frames the nondeterminism problem from the production-inference angle. Skim for the core argument and the proposed solution.

→ Blog post
READ C3 90 min

zkLLM or VeriLLM

Pick whichever is more current

Goal: understand what zero-knowledge proofs of inference can actually deliver today (in overhead terms) and what is still aspirational.

Specific paper assigned with track confirmation.

Track D · Workload classification from hardware telemetry

For people drawn to side-channel analysis: can you determine what a chip is doing from power, EM, memory access patterns, or network traffic alone.

READ D1 30 min

AI Security Forum RFD 001 — Side-Channel Leakage from LLM Inference

Gabriel Kulp (2024)

The framing document. Power, EM, acoustic, thermal. Which side-channel is most promising for distinguishing inference from training, and why no one has published the experiment yet.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-3.
READ D2 60 min

Input-Dependent Power Usage in GPUs

Gregersen et al.

The starting empirical evidence that GPU workload is recoverable from power traces. Read for the methodology, not the specific results. The techniques transfer to AI workload classification.

Link circulated with track assignment.
READ D3 45 min

AI Security Forum RFD 002 — MoE Expert Fingerprinting

Gabriel Kulp (2024)

The cleverer attack vector. MoE routing creates distinctive computational signatures. If you can fingerprint them, you can recover token sequences from physical observations alone.

Link circulated via cohort email at T-3.

Track E · Your own idea (cross-cutting)

For people proposing their own project (tile 05 on the programme page). Week 2 looks different — you ground your proposal in the field's existing structure and pick adjacent readings to fill gaps.

READ E1 60 min

Open Problems in Technical AI Governance

Reuel, Hardy, Smith et al. (2024)

The Stanford catalogue. Use it to ground your own proposal in the field's existing structure. Make sure you are not proposing something already named as a known-unsolved problem without realising.

→ Paper on arXiv (2407.14981)
READ E2 + E3 Variable

Two readings from adjacent tracks

Picked with the programme team

Email the programme team your project sketch and ask for reading suggestions by end of Week 2. We will pick two from the other tracks that fill the gaps most relevant to your proposal.

Email hello@cambridgeaisafety.org with your sketch.
Week 3 · T-2 · Adjacent Depth + Discussion

Adjacent Depth + Discussion

Two things happen this week. First, you read one paper outside your track to broaden context. Second, you participate in a small-group discussion call with 3-4 other participants from adjacent tracks, plus one resident.

Adjacent reading

READ 05 45–60 min Outside your track

Pick one reading from a different track

Your choice from Track A, B, C, or D readings

The goal is to be conversational about a problem area you are not working on, so the cohort can think across tracks during the programme week. Pick one that sits closest to your existing background but in a different track.

READ 06 30 min Optional

Field convergence points

HAP internal summary

A short summary of where the field broadly agrees something is unsolved — proof-of-training, on-chip FLOP metering, multi-cluster training detection. These are strong candidates for projects that produce lasting work after the week.

Summary circulated via cohort email at T-2.
Small-group discussion · 90 minutes

Discussion call

Groups of 4-5 participants plus one resident, organised by adjacency rather than identical tracks. Come prepared with written notes (2-3 sentences each) on these prompts. The discussion uses them as starting points rather than as a checklist to burn through.

  • What's the most interesting thing you've read so far?
  • Where is your track's strongest published result, and what's the most important thing still unsolved?
  • What experiment would you most want to run, given access to a frontier data centre?
  • What are you scared the cohort week will miss?
Week 4 · T-1 · Landing

Landing

Light. The goal of Week 4 is that by the time you land in Cambridge on Sunday 16 August, you are ready to work — not still reading.

READ 07 ~60 min total Resident work primers

One short piece per resident

Curated by the programme team

Read one short piece by each resident to know who you are working with. Plan for ~10 minutes per resident. The programme team will send a curated set the week before arrival.

Resident primer set circulated via cohort email at T-1.
READ 08 15 min Logistics

Programme handbook

HAP programme team

Day-by-day schedule, Cambridge logistics (arrival, accommodation at Wilde, venue, meals), dev environment notes per track, what to bring and what not to.

Handbook circulated via cohort email at T-1.
Day 1 prep

Day 1 checklist

One-page checklist for the morning of 17 August. Laptop ready, accommodation confirmed, any signed forms returned. Light admin only — most of the prep is already done by this point.
